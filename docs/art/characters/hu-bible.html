Hu — the kid from 仙麓

The starstruck pilgrim of 九仙山. Nobody's disciple — which is the entire point.

一Backstory — 仙麓 and the 奈何客棧

Hu grew up in 仙麓 — "the Immortals' Foothill," the village at the base of the pilgrim road — raised at his aunt's inn, the 奈何客棧. The inn's name is the village's whole humor in two characters: what can you do? Pilgrims laugh at it on the way up. Nobody laughs at it on the way down.

仙麓 lives off the mountain: sells climbers straw sandals and courage going up, buys their gear back cheap when they come down done. Hu grew up on what climbers left behind — cracked scabbards, lucky charms, half a rack of returned weapons on the inn's back wall, and above all stories, told at the long table by people who had sparred with the Nine's disciples and come back changed in ways the boy only later learned to count.

Never taken as a disciple — schools want lineage or tuition — he learned the only way left: watching through fences, and practicing with whatever the buy-back wall had that week. Sword one month, staff the next, a spear with a cracked haft all winter.

His kung fu is a patchwork of imitations, learned wrong, in the honest way — the only kung fu on the mountain that doesn't descend from the door.

Twice he saved coin for a school's gate fee; twice he was laughed off before the inner door. At sixteen-and-change he did the thing every 仙麓 kid jokes about and no one does: packed his aunt's worst blanket, took a weapon off the wall, and started climbing — to ask the Nine themselves.

二The mountain he finds — warm, courteous, wrong

The climb is everything he dreamed — at first. The mountain is warm and proper: disciples bow before sparring, masters correct his footwork mid-bout and mean it kindly, shrines offer tea. Fights are sparring matches with real stakes — the jianghu's way of talking. Hu is welcomed, taught, happy.

The wrongness arrives as anomalies, not menace: climbers he met at the 奈何客棧 never come down — or come down abnormal, smiling at nothing. A master mid-lesson says a sentence in the wrong order, twice, identically. The higher shrines are clean. Too clean.

The politeness never breaks. That becomes the horror.

The real face is meta-gated: only after Hu achieves what the surface story asks — the false final bosses across runs, the key relics — does the mountain show what it is. (Narrative-structure slice; the within-run altitude bands stay underneath.)

三The arc — curious → humorous → calm

Early — curious, humorous

"Wait — Crane school?? What's HE like??"
— mid-spar, to an opponent genuinely trying to win

"That was Sleeve Wind! …I think. The left-handed one. Sorry about your ribs."
— names techniques out loud, slightly wrong, with delight; apologizes after winning

Middle — the counting

"You said that already. Word for word. …Anyway — again?"
— the jokes continue, but he's writing things in the stamp book that aren't autographs

Late — calm, master-like

"Put it down. You can, you know."
— short sentences; kindness without eagerness; he corrects a young disciple's footwork mid-bout and means it kindly — the full circle

The final inversion stands: from "teach me" to "let go of everything they taught me." The buildless true fight is his life's thesis — the manner he worshipped, arrived at down a road none of the Nine took.

四The buy-back arsenal — weapons

Hu is weapon-broad because the inn's wall dictated his curriculum. This grounds the future weapon system: each run he takes one weapon from the wall; each type has variants, plus one hidden legendary variant per type. The wall grows toward the 十八般兵器. Chapter 1 ships the sword only — and the fiction of the wall.

贗劍

The Replica Sword — chapter 1

A souvenir-row "Genuine Ninth Immortal Replica." Proportions slightly off. The vendor's red tassel-tag still on the hilt — he can't bring himself to cut it. Hip-carried, humble scabbard.

Every authentic blade descends from the door's lineage. The fake is the only clean sword on the mountain — as Hu stains, stage by stage, the replica never does. An ink-veined hand on an untouched hilt.

集印簿

The Stamp Book

A pilgrim's shrine-stamp book, repurposed: shrine stamps, ticket stubs, two school refusal slips kept out of spite — and nine empty slots he ruled himself, one per immortal autograph. Belt-worn, oilcloth-wrapped.

Its pages darken with the climb; in the middle arc it becomes his anomaly ledger. What fills the nine slots is never what he hoped.
